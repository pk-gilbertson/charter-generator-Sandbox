01

Before you start your charter

You do not need every answer finalized before you begin. A clear first draft is more useful than waiting for perfect wording. The goal is to create a practical starting point that can be reviewed and refined.

02

Start with a few core decisions

Users do not need every item fully finalized, but it helps to begin with an initial point of view on the items below.

Committee identity

  • Proposed charter name
  • Committee type and scope
  • Organizational level or cross-agency reach

Leadership and sponsorship

  • Sponsor
  • Chair or lead

Purpose and boundaries

  • Purpose of the group
  • In-scope activities
  • Out-of-scope activities

Authority and participation

  • Decision authority or escalation path
  • Expected members and required perspectives

Operating approach

  • Meeting cadence
  • Decision-making process
  • Meeting administration and recordkeeping

03

Use starter content to get moving

Starter content can help you begin quickly. It provides a practical example, but it should be tailored so the draft reflects the real responsibilities, decisions, and operating style of your group.

Use starter content to

  • Break the blank-page problem
  • See what a complete draft structure looks like
  • Accelerate first-pass drafting

Do not use starter content to

  • Replace real decisions about scope or authority
  • Assume every committee should operate the same way
  • Skip review and refinement with stakeholders

04

A good first draft is better than a perfect blank page

Focus on clarity, practicality, and reviewability. The goal is to give sponsors, reviewers, and participants something concrete to react to and improve.
